I am a PhD researcher in the [Fagan Lab](faganlab.com) at the University of Sheffield, funded through the MRC DiMeN Doctoral Training Partnership. My research focuses on the biology of bacteriophages infecting Clostridioides difficile — one of the most clinically significant nosocomial pathogens globally. I am particularly interested in phage-host interactions at the molecular level: how phages integrate into and excise from the C. difficile chromosome, how they remodel host physiology during infection, and how their structural proteins can be engineered for therapeutic or diagnostic applications.

A central component of my work involves cryo-electron microscopy for high-resolution structural characterisation of C. difficile phages, complemented by bacterial genetics, molecular cloning, and bioinformatics approaches including large-scale comparative genomics across thousands of clinical isolates. I develop both wet-lab and computational tools to interrogate phage biology in ways that are directly relevant to the clinical problem of C. difficile infection.

Beyond my doctoral research, I am committed to translating biological insight into tangible healthcare solutions. I serve as a scientific advisor to the University of Sheffield iGEM team, where I guide student-led projects at the intersection of synthetic biology and medicine — most recently on engineered exosome-based delivery systems targeting the central nervous system.
I completed my undergraduate degree at the National Institute of Science Education and Research (NISER), Bhubaneswar, and conducted research at the Max Planck Institute for Evolutionary Biology prior to my doctoral studies.
